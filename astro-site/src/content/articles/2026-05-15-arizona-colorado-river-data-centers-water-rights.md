## The Ecological Development Brief

The Colorado River is overallocated. That's not a contested claim — it's been the legal and hydrological reality since 1922, when the Colorado River Compact divided 17.5 million acre-feet of water among seven states based on flow measurements that turned out to reflect an unusually wet period. The river's long-term average flow is closer to 13 to 14 million acre-feet. For a hundred years, the gap between what was promised and what exists has been papered over by reservoir storage, renegotiated agreements, and the refusal of downstream states to acknowledge what the hydrology clearly shows.

Lake Mead hit its lowest recorded level in 2022. It's recovered partially since, but the structural deficit hasn't changed. Arizona, Nevada, and California were forced to accept cuts in their junior water right allocations. Arizona farmers in Pinal County watched their water allocations reduced and, in some cases, eliminated entirely. The state's 2023 Drought Contingency Plan has farmers fallowing fields — leaving them unplanted and unirrigated — in exchange for conservation payments, because there simply isn't enough water.

At the same time, Arizona is actively marketing itself to the data center industry. The Arizona Commerce Authority's recruitment materials list the state's available land, its tax incentives, its fiber infrastructure, and its power costs. Water is mentioned as available. The state has approved data center developments in the Phoenix metro — one of the fastest-growing data center markets in the country — that use evaporative cooling systems drawing from the Salt River Project and Central Arizona Project water systems. The CAP is fed by the Colorado River. The water being evaporated by a Phoenix data center cooling tower and the water that an Arizona farmer couldn't put on his cotton field this year come from the same place.

## Water & Cooling Impact Analysis

Evaporative cooling in the desert Southwest consumes more water per unit of cooling than in any other climate region in the country. The thermodynamic reason is the dry-bulb to wet-bulb temperature differential: in Phoenix, where summer ambient air is hot and dry, the cooling tower must evaporate large volumes of water to achieve the necessary heat rejection because the temperature gradient driving evaporation is high. A hyperscale campus consuming 2 million gallons of water per day in Virginia might consume 3 to 4 million gallons per day running the same IT load under evaporative cooling in the Phoenix summer.

Phoenix is not the only Arizona market. Goodyear, Chandler, Mesa, and the East Valley have all seen significant data center development. Each of those facilities draws from water systems that are directly or indirectly connected to Colorado River allocations through the CAP or the Salt River Project's reservoir system. The CAP, which delivers Colorado River water to central and southern Arizona, is the lifeblood of the Phoenix metro's water supply. Arizona's CAP allocation is 1.57 million acre-feet per year — the largest single state allocation in the Colorado River Compact. It is also the most junior: under the prior appropriation doctrine, Arizona's CAP allocation is the first to be cut when river flows are insufficient to meet all demands. It has already been cut. It will likely be cut again.

A data center signed to a 20-year lease in Chandler in 2025 is committing its CAP-sourced water consumption to that footprint for two decades — through what climate projections uniformly suggest will be a period of continued and deepening aridification in the Colorado River basin. The Colorado River Compact's overallocation isn't getting better. It's getting worse. The data center that signed that 20-year lease will still be there, still cooling with evaporated Colorado River water, when the river's conditions have deteriorated further from where they are today.

## Power Grid & Emissions Profile

Arizona's grid is cleaner than Virginia's but not as clean as Iowa's. The state has a significant solar generation base — appropriate given the solar resource — and has been retiring coal capacity, including the closure of the Navajo Generating Station in 2019. The remaining generation mix includes natural gas peakers that run hard during summer afternoon load peaks, when air conditioning demand from 115-degree Phoenix heat coincides with peak data center compute loads. The summer afternoon period is when Arizona's grid carbon intensity is highest, when water demand is highest, and when data center cooling systems are working hardest. All three stresses peak simultaneously.

The tax incentive structure Arizona uses to attract data centers is similar to Iowa's: sales tax exemptions on equipment, property tax abatements, occasionally subsidized power rates. Arizona's incentive program has no water use restriction provisions. I checked the ACA's program documentation. There are no water efficiency requirements, no evaporative cooling restrictions, no preference for air-side economization or liquid cooling that would reduce water consumption in a water-scarce environment. The state is competing for data center investment on cost — power cost, tax cost, land cost — without incorporating the cost of water scarcity into the economic calculation that drives location decisions.

## Land Conservation & Community Impact

The Pinal County farmers fallowing their fields while Phoenix data centers draw from the same river system aren't just a policy irony. They're a demonstration of what water market economics look like when industrial users with larger capital bases compete against agricultural users for the same finite resource. Data center operators can afford to pay more per acre-foot for water than cotton farmers can. That's not a moral failure — it's market mechanics. But it means that water policy that leaves data center development outside the scarcity framework governing agricultural water use is effectively choosing a winner in that competition without saying so publicly.

Arizona's water managers are sophisticated. The Arizona Department of Water Resources has managed the state's groundwater and surface water systems under complex legal frameworks for decades. But the data center buildout has arrived faster than the policy frameworks have adapted, and the gap between agricultural water management — tightly regulated, annually reviewed, subject to shortage declarations and mandatory cuts — and data center water use — essentially unregulated in terms of conservation requirements — is widening with every new campus announcement.

The Colorado River basin is a system that cannot deliver everything that has been promised to everyone who has been promised it. That's been true since 1922. What's new is that the list of promisees now includes data centers cooling AI inference workloads, and nobody in Phoenix or Sacramento or Denver is accounting for that demand in the basin-wide water budget. The accounting matters. The river doesn't grow to meet demand. It doesn't care how important the workload is.
