## The Ecological Development Brief

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — PFAS, often called forever chemicals — have a half-life in the human body measured in years and a half-life in the environment that, for some compounds, is effectively infinite. They don't biodegrade under natural conditions. They bioaccumulate in fatty tissues up trophic levels. They cross the placental barrier and appear in breast milk. And they are present in the fire suppression systems of virtually every large data center built before 2020, in the form of aqueous film-forming foam — AFFF — which contains fluorotelomer-based PFAS compounds as its active fire-suppressing ingredient.

I want to be precise about what I'm claiming and what I'm not. I'm not claiming that data centers are routinely discharging PFAS-contaminated foam into the environment. AFFF suppression systems are designed to activate only in fire events, and large-scale suppressant releases are rare. What I am claiming is that: PFAS compounds are present in these systems at data center sites; the soil and groundwater monitoring that would detect their migration to surrounding areas from storage, maintenance activities, testing, and incidental release doesn't exist as a standard practice for data center operators; and the regulatory framework that applies to PFAS at data center sites is substantially weaker than the framework that applies to military bases and airports, which are where most of the documented PFAS contamination from AFFF has occurred.

The data center industry is now one of the larger industrial users of AFFF suppression systems by facility count. That fact doesn't appear in the PFAS policy conversation. It should.

## Water & Cooling Impact Analysis

PFAS contamination pathways at data center sites include more than just fire suppression. Some cooling water treatment formulations have historically contained PFAS-based surfactants as corrosion inhibitors and wetting agents — though industry has been moving away from these following PFAS regulatory pressure. The transition is incomplete. Formulations in use at older facilities, and in some cases at newer ones where procurement documentation doesn't include PFAS screening, may still contain fluorinated compounds.

The toxicological profile of PFAS compounds is, at this point, well-characterized in the primary literature. PFOA and PFOS — the longest-chain compounds, now largely phased out but still present in legacy systems — bind to serum albumin in blood, accumulate in the liver, and are associated with thyroid disruption, immune suppression, and reproductive toxicity at environmentally relevant concentrations. The EPA's 2022 health advisory set the limit for PFOA in drinking water at 0.004 parts per trillion — a number that reflects the agency's determination that there is no safe level of exposure to this compound. Four parts per quadrillion. That's the standard.

Groundwater contamination from AFFF at military bases and airports has produced PFAS plumes extending miles from the source. The soil chemistry that drives that migration — PFAS compounds are highly mobile in sandy and loamy soils because their fluorinated carbon chain doesn't bind well to soil organic matter — is the same chemistry at data center sites. The difference is that military base contamination was identified through systematic monitoring required by federal law. Data center sites aren't subject to equivalent monitoring requirements. Whether PFAS compounds are migrating from data center AFFF systems into surrounding groundwater and from there into private wells and municipal water sources is a question that hasn't been asked systematically — because no regulatory framework currently requires anyone to ask it.

## Power Grid & Emissions Profile

The shift away from AFFF toward fluorine-free firefighting foam — F3 — is underway in the data center industry, driven partly by regulatory pressure from the EU and several U.S. states and partly by voluntary commitments from major operators. Google, Microsoft, and Meta have all announced timelines for transitioning their suppression systems to F3. The transitions are real and they're the right direction. But they're happening at different paces at different facilities, and the retrofitting of existing AFFF systems is expensive, which means older or smaller facilities are lagging the commitments made at the corporate level.

The F3 transition also doesn't address the legacy contamination question. If PFAS compounds from existing AFFF systems have already migrated into soil and groundwater at data center sites — from past fire events, from maintenance activities, from foam concentrate storage — transitioning to F3 going forward doesn't remediate what's already there. PFAS remediation in soil and groundwater is technically difficult and extremely expensive. The standard approach — pump-and-treat — has a measured effectiveness for PFAS that is lower than for most other contaminants because PFAS compounds rebind to soil particles and re-release into groundwater over long timescales. The contamination, once established, is genuinely difficult to reverse.

## Land Conservation & Community Impact

The communities surrounding data center campuses — particularly in secondary and tertiary markets where facilities are often sited near residential areas and where private well use is more common than in dense urban areas — have no way of knowing whether PFAS from a nearby data center site has reached their water supply. Municipal water systems are now required to test for PFAS under the EPA's April 2024 final rule, which set maximum contaminant levels for six PFAS compounds. Private wells are not covered by that rule. Private well owners must test their own water at their own expense and know to ask for the test — knowledge and resources that are not uniformly distributed across the communities that host rural and suburban data center sites.

I'd like to see data center operators commit to perimeter groundwater monitoring at all sites with AFFF systems, with results published annually in a format accessible to surrounding communities. That monitoring should extend to private wells within a specified radius of the site, with operators bearing the cost of testing and remediation if contamination is detected. This is what military installations are required to do under CERCLA and the National Defense Authorization Acts. There is no logical reason why a data center campus with the same AFFF suppression technology should be subject to weaker monitoring requirements than an Air Force base.

The forever-chemical problem at data center sites is not theoretical. The chemicals are there. The migration pathways exist. The monitoring doesn't. Until that changes, communities near these facilities are living next to an uncharacterized contamination risk that nobody is required to characterize.
