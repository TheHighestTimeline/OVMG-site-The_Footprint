## The Ecological Development Brief

When Google announced a $1.2 billion AI campus in Columbus, Ohio, coverage split into two frames: jobs and GPUs. The H100 clusters. The 4,200 construction roles. The community college partnership. All real. All worth reporting. But not one outlet mentioned the Scioto River — which drains the exact watershed this campus sits in — or the freshwater mussel species listed under the Endangered Species Act that depend on it. I looked. The Scioto doesn't appear in any of the coverage I found.

Why does that matter? Because a 200-megawatt AI campus isn't primarily an employment story. It's a thermal engine. It consumes electrical energy, converts most of it to heat, and pushes that heat into the surrounding environment — through evaporative cooling towers, wastewater discharge, and condenser systems. Where that heat goes determines the biological consequences. The Scioto watershed is where it goes. And the mussel species in that system can't relocate when conditions change. They're sessile. They absorb what arrives.

This pattern isn't unique to Columbus. Iowa, Indiana, and Nebraska have collectively seen over $800 million in data center investment announcements in the past 18 months — all following the same template. Job count. Capital figure. Utility partnership. Silence on the biological systems the facility will interact with for the next 30 years. My concern isn't that these campuses shouldn't exist. It's that we're making 30-year ecological commitments without the baseline data that would let us understand what we're committing to.

## Water & Cooling Impact Analysis

The Scioto River basin supports one of the more biodiverse freshwater mussel assemblages remaining in the upper Ohio River drainage. That's not a romantic characterization — it's documented in ODNR surveys and U.S. Fish & Wildlife assessments. Roughly 70% of North America's approximately 300 native mussel species are listed as endangered, threatened, or of special concern. Freshwater mussels are among the most imperiled organisms on the continent. And they're acutely sensitive to exactly what a hyperscale campus delivers: thermal loading and chemical contamination from cooling system discharge.

Here's the biology. Mussels are sessile filter feeders. They can't move. Their thermal tolerance windows are narrow — most species begin showing measurable physiological stress at temperatures just 3 to 5 degrees Celsius above their acclimation point. What does that stress look like? Heat shock protein induction. Reduced filtration rate. Impaired gametogenesis. Cooling tower blowdown entering municipal wastewater carries residual heat plus biocides and corrosion inhibitors. Municipal treatment systems aren't designed to remove either before discharge to the receiving stream. During summer low-flow periods — when dilution capacity drops and ambient temperatures are already at their peak — cumulative loading from multiple industrial users can push conditions past physiological tolerance thresholds for the species present.

Google hasn't disclosed the cooling method, daily water withdrawal volume, or discharge chemistry for this facility. I checked the EIS filings. I checked the press releases. I checked the Ohio EPA permit database. Three of the four biological parameters I'd need to begin assessing the actual impact on the Scioto system are simply not there. The fourth — a pre-construction biological baseline survey of the mussel assemblage — isn't required under Ohio data center permitting. Which means we may end up with a facility that causes measurable harm to a listed species and no pre-disturbance data to establish what changed. That's not an accident. It's a permitting gap.

## Power Grid & Emissions Profile

Ohio extended $200 million in property tax abatements to land this campus. The local debate framed it purely as a fiscal question: does payroll tax from 850 permanent roles offset the foregone revenue? The math isn't unreasonable — those roles generate roughly $18,000 each in annual state and local taxes, about $15.3 million per year. Over 20 years, the state probably comes out ahead.

But that calculation treats environmental carrying costs as externalities. Water infrastructure upgrades, transmission expansion, land conversion — none of it in the model. AEP Ohio has indicated it needs to build new transmission to serve the Columbus data center corridor. That infrastructure runs through agricultural land and, in places, approaches riparian corridors along Scioto tributaries where vegetation buffers are thin. Transmission right-of-way corridors require clearing, create compacted surface beneath them, and alter hydrology in ways that affect groundwater recharge and stream connectivity. The ecological footprint of a data center extends to the grid it requires. That extended footprint hasn't been assessed.

And AEP Ohio's generation mix still includes meaningful coal capacity as of 2024. The electrons physically flowing into this campus on a cloudy January day reflect that mix — the NOx, SO2, particulate matter entering the airshed and the watershed through wet and dry deposition. Google purchases renewable energy certificates to decouple the carbon accounting from the physical generation. That's a financial instrument. The respiratory systems of communities near the generating plants don't have access to it.

## Land Conservation & Community Impact

The 4,200 construction jobs are real. The wages — $55,000 to $95,000 in a $52,000-median market — represent genuine economic mobility. I don't dismiss that. But economic benefit and ecological accountability aren't substitutes. The coverage has treated them as if they are, as if a job count answers questions that are actually about biology. It doesn't.

The community college partnership is the most genuinely thoughtful element of the announcement — though not for the reason it usually gets cited. Training a local workforce for data center operations creates people with a stake in how the facility performs long-term. An electrician who lives downstream of the cooling tower discharge has a different relationship to its environmental performance than a contractor flown in for commissioning. That kind of embeddedness matters for accountability in ways that are hard to quantify but real.

What I'd ask of Google — and every operator building at this scale in the Midwest — is straightforward: pre-operational biological baseline survey of the receiving watershed. Post-operational monitoring at defined intervals, publicly disclosed. Cooling chemistry and daily withdrawal volumes as routine disclosures. Not onerous. Not expensive relative to a $1.2 billion campus. Just the minimum data collection needed to know whether the ecological commitment being made here is one the Scioto watershed can actually absorb. Right now, we don't have that data. We're making the commitment anyway.
