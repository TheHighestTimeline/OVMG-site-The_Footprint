## The Ecological Development Brief

Loudoun County, Virginia added more data center square footage in 2025 than any other jurisdiction on earth. That sentence gets repeated a lot in the industry press, usually with pride. What doesn't get repeated: Loudoun County and the surrounding jurisdictions drew approximately 28 billion gallons from the Potomac River system last year, and data centers accounted for a meaningful share of that industrial demand that no one has publicly quantified with precision.

I've been trying to find that number for three months. It's not in the Loudoun Water annual report. It's not in NVRC's regional water supply planning documents. The Northern Virginia Regional Commission tracks water demand by sector — residential, commercial, industrial — but data centers get folded into commercial and industrial categories in ways that make facility-level attribution impossible. That opacity isn't accidental. It's a permitting and reporting framework that was designed before the Ashburn corridor became the densest concentration of data center infrastructure on the planet.

Dominion Energy has now imposed a de facto interconnection moratorium on new large-load customers in the region. PJM's queue runs past 2030. Those constraints are getting covered extensively as an infrastructure and real estate story — campus pipelines slowing, hyperscalers looking at other markets. The water constraint is getting covered almost nowhere. Both are real. Only one of them recovers when you build more transmission lines.

## Water & Cooling Impact Analysis

The Potomac River system supplies drinking water to roughly 6 million people in the DC metro area. The Washington Aqueduct, which serves DC, Arlington, and Falls Church, draws from the Potomac. The Occoquan Reservoir, which serves much of Fairfax County and Prince William, is recharged from the Occoquan watershed that the data center corridor sits above. The relationship between industrial cooling water demand in Loudoun and the long-term yield capacity of the Occoquan system isn't a hypothetical. It's a water planning question that regional authorities are actively managing — just not in connection with the data center buildout that represents the fastest-growing industrial water demand in the watershed.

Evaporative cooling at hyperscale draws water continuously. A single large campus at 100+ megawatts can consume 1 to 2 million gallons per day during summer peak conditions. Multiply that across the 35-plus hyperscale campuses operating in the Ashburn corridor and the surrounding Loudoun footprint. The regional water demand figure gets significant fast — and it has to come from somewhere during drought years, when the Potomac's flow drops and the Occoquan's recharge rate slows. Virginia experienced below-normal precipitation in three of the last five years. The Potomac Conservancy has flagged increasing stress on the low-flow summer conditions that determine how much water the system can actually allocate.

None of the environmental review processes for individual data center campuses in Loudoun require a cumulative watershed impact assessment. Each facility gets permitted on its own, against its own projected demand, without anyone being required to add up what all the facilities together are drawing from the same system. That's the gap. It's not that individual facilities are out of compliance. It's that the compliance framework was never built to account for what happens when you put 35 of them in the same watershed.

## Power Grid & Emissions Profile

The interconnection moratorium has a silver lining that nobody's talking about: it's slowing the buildout in a market where the grid is among the most carbon-intensive in the Mid-Atlantic, partly because of coal and gas generation that serves Northern Virginia's baseload demand. Dominion's generation mix has been improving — more solar in the portfolio, more offshore wind coming — but the speed of data center load growth has consistently outpaced the pace of grid decarbonization in this market. Slowing the build rate, even involuntarily, gives the grid mix time to catch up.

The buildout isn't stopping. It's relocating. Data center campus announcements in the past six months have increasingly pointed toward secondary Virginia markets — Prince William County, Fauquier County, the I-81 corridor — where land is cheaper and interconnection queues are shorter. Those secondary markets sit in different watersheds. The Shenandoah River system. The Rappahannock. Each with its own water stress profile, its own ecology, its own communities who have not been asked whether they want to become the next Ashburn. They're finding out the same way Loudoun found out — one special use permit at a time.

## Land Conservation & Community Impact

The communities in the path of the secondary market expansion deserve a warning that Loudoun didn't get. By the time Loudoun County understood what it had become — the global epicenter of data center density — the buildout had a decade of momentum and an assessed valuation that made any serious course correction politically impossible. The tax revenue from data centers now represents a structural dependency in the county budget. You don't easily vote against your largest taxpayer.

Fauquier County, which sits to the west of the Loudoun corridor in the northern Shenandoah foothills, has been fielding data center inquiries for 18 months. It still has time to make deliberate decisions — to require cumulative watershed impact assessments, to set water withdrawal limits as a condition of special use permits, to engage the communities in the Rappahannock headwaters before the first shovel breaks ground. Whether it will is a different question. The tax revenue math is persuasive, and the consultants who bring these deals don't usually lead with the watershed impacts.

The sharpest thing I can say about Northern Virginia is this: a region that built the backbone of the global internet without once doing a cumulative water impact assessment across its data center corridor is not a model to replicate. It's a cautionary tale that the secondary markets are about to live through in fast-forward.
