## The Ecological Development Brief

Amazon announced in 2023 that it had reached 100% renewable energy across its global operations. It was the fastest any company had ever hit that milestone at that scale. The press coverage was effusive. The stock moved. The sustainability reports glowed.

Here's what 100% renewable energy actually means when Amazon says it: the company purchased enough renewable energy certificates to match its total global electricity consumption on an annual basis. That's it. The electrons physically flowing into AWS data centers in Northern Virginia on a Tuesday night in January are generated by whatever is on the grid at that moment — coal, gas, nuclear, wind, whatever Dominion Energy's dispatch algorithm determines is cheapest and most available. The REC says that somewhere, at some point during that year, an equivalent amount of renewable generation happened. The certificate and the electron are legally decoupled. They don't have to occur in the same place, the same hour, or even the same season.

I'm not saying Amazon is lying. I'm saying the metric doesn't measure what most people think it measures. And when a company with AWS's scale uses that metric to claim environmental leadership, it's worth understanding precisely what the claim does and doesn't cover.

## Water & Cooling Impact Analysis

Amazon's data center water consumption is one of the least-disclosed numbers in the industry for a company of its size. The company publishes a water intensity metric — gallons consumed per dollar of revenue — but not total water withdrawal volumes at the facility or regional level. I've looked at their sustainability reports going back four years. Total water withdrawal: not there. Regional breakdown: not there. Watershed-level impact assessments: not there.

What can be estimated: AWS operates several hundred data centers globally, concentrated heavily in Northern Virginia, Ohio, Oregon, and Ireland. Conservative industry estimates for hyperscale water consumption run 1 to 3 million gallons per day per large campus under evaporative cooling. AWS has more than 30 significant campus footprints in Northern Virginia alone. The cumulative daily withdrawal from the Potomac and Occoquan systems attributable to AWS facilities is almost certainly in the hundreds of millions of gallons annually. Amazon has not published that number. Their 100% renewable announcement got a press release. Their water footprint gets a ratio in a table on page 47.

That asymmetry — loud claims on the metric that looks good, silence on the metric that doesn't — is what greenwashing actually looks like in practice. It's not usually fabrication. It's selection. Which numbers get published, which get buried in ratios, and which never get calculated at all.

## Power Grid & Emissions Profile

The 24/7 clean energy standard — pioneered by Google and now being adopted by a growing number of operators — is the right benchmark, and Amazon hasn't met it. The difference between annual matching and hourly matching is significant. Annual matching lets a company purchase solar generation in July and apply it against server load in January. Hourly matching requires clean generation to be available in the same hour as the load — which means storage, nuclear, or geographically distributed renewable portfolios that can cover periods when sun and wind aren't generating. That's a much harder and more expensive standard to meet. Amazon's REC-based 100% claim doesn't attempt it.

Amazon Web Services runs at higher utilization rates than most enterprise data centers — a genuine efficiency advantage. The company has also made substantial renewable energy investments, including large solar and wind PPAs across multiple markets. Those investments are real and they matter. But PPA-matched renewable generation that occurs somewhere in Texas doesn't decarbonize the electrons running AI inference workloads in Ashburn, Virginia at 3 a.m. The grid physics don't work that way. The accounting framework does — which is exactly the problem.

AWS's Scope 2 emissions under the market-based method, using RECs, come out very low. AWS's Scope 2 emissions under the location-based method — using the actual carbon intensity of the grids where their facilities physically operate — are substantially higher. Both numbers are in the sustainability report. Amazon leads with the market-based figure. The location-based figure is on a different page, in a smaller table, with less context. That's not an accident of layout.

## Land Conservation & Community Impact

Amazon's land footprint from data center campuses is among the largest of any technology company and among the least-discussed from an ecological standpoint. Each campus requires site clearing, grading, stormwater infrastructure, and years of construction activity that alters the hydrology of the surrounding land. The company doesn't publish a consolidated land use figure or a habitat impact assessment. The individual facility environmental reviews, where they exist, sit in local permitting records scattered across dozens of jurisdictions. Nobody has added them up. Amazon certainly hasn't.

The 100% renewable announcement generated hundreds of articles. The water footprint generates almost none. The land use generates almost none. That coverage gap is partly Amazon's communications strategy working as intended — release the metric that looks best, keep the others in the footnotes — and partly a press that hasn't figured out how to cover environmental accountability as rigorously as it covers earnings.

What I want from Amazon is the same thing I want from every operator at this scale: total annual water withdrawal by region, broken down by facility size, published alongside the renewable energy claims. Not a ratio. Not an intensity figure. The number. Gallons per year, by watershed. Until that's a standard disclosure, the 100% renewable headline is doing work it hasn't earned.
