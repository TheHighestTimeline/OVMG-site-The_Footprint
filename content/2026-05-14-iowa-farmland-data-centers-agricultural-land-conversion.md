## The Ecological Development Brief

Iowa has some of the best agricultural soil on earth. Class I and Class II soils — the highest-productivity classifications in the USDA's land capability scheme — cover significant portions of the state's central corridor. These soils represent thousands of years of organic matter accumulation, microbial community development, and hydrological function. They're also, increasingly, where data center campuses are being built.

Iowa has approved more than $600 million in data center tax exemptions over the past three years, under a program that exempts data center equipment purchases from state sales tax in exchange for capital investment and job commitments. The facilities that those incentives are attracting are landing disproportionately on agricultural land — because that's where the large, flat, accessible parcels are in a state where urban land is constrained and rural zoning is permissive. The tax incentive program has no provision requiring that data centers avoid prime agricultural soils. I looked. It doesn't.

Meta has a major campus in Altoona. Microsoft is building in West Des Moines. Google has significant infrastructure in Council Bluffs. Each of those sites required converting agricultural land — in some cases Class I and II soils — to impervious surface. Once concrete goes down on Class I agricultural soil, the agronomic and ecological productivity of that land is gone on any human-relevant timescale. The microbial community that built that soil over centuries can't be relocated. It can't be restored in 10 years or 20 years. The impervious surface persists for the life of the facility, which is measured in decades, and the soil disturbance persists effectively permanently.

## Water & Cooling Impact Analysis

Iowa draws its cooling water primarily from municipal water systems and, in some configurations, from the Des Moines River and its tributaries. The Des Moines River watershed is one of the most nutrient-loaded in the country — agricultural runoff has elevated nitrogen and phosphorus concentrations well above natural baseline levels, contributing to hypoxic conditions in the Gulf of Mexico via the Mississippi River system. Data center cooling discharge in the Des Moines watershed adds thermal and chemical loading to a system that's already carrying a heavy nutrient burden.

Meta's Altoona campus, which is one of the larger single-site data center footprints in the country, has not publicly disclosed its daily water withdrawal from the regional system or its discharge chemistry. I've asked. The response was a reference to their sustainability report, which contains a global water intensity figure. Not a volume. Not a watershed. A ratio. When I followed up asking for the Altoona-specific figure, I got no response.

The soil conversion question has a water dimension that isn't obvious until you think through the hydrology. Agricultural land, even intensively farmed land, retains some capacity for rainfall infiltration and groundwater recharge. Impervious surface — parking lots, building foundations, sealed roads — eliminates that capacity entirely. Rainfall that previously infiltrated and recharged the local aquifer now runs off as stormwater, carrying whatever it picks up on the surface. Data center campuses in Iowa have been large enough that their stormwater footprint measurably changes the hydrology of the surrounding parcels. That altered hydrology affects neighboring farms' drainage patterns, affects the recharge rate of the shallow aquifer system that many Iowa farms draw irrigation water from, and eventually affects surface water quality through altered runoff timing and volume.

## Power Grid & Emissions Profile

Iowa gets roughly 60% of its electricity from wind — the highest wind penetration of any state in the country. That's the reason data center operators give for choosing Iowa, and it's a genuine advantage. The grid here is substantially cleaner than the Dominion territory serving Northern Virginia, and it's getting cleaner. MidAmerican Energy, which serves most of the Des Moines metro, has committed to 100% renewable generation and is well on its way. That's a real and meaningful difference from markets where coal and gas dominate the dispatch stack.

But the renewable energy story doesn't resolve the land and water story. A data center powered entirely by Iowa wind still sits on land that was agricultural and is now not. It still draws water from a river system that's already nutrient-stressed. It still discharges cooling chemistry into a municipal wastewater system that empties into the Des Moines watershed. The power source is one variable. It's not the only one, and in the coverage of the Iowa data center buildout, it has become the variable that ends the environmental discussion before the other variables get addressed.

The jobs argument in Iowa is weaker than in Ohio or Virginia. Iowa has a labor shortage, not a labor surplus. The permanent operations roles these facilities create — typically 50 to 150 positions for a large campus — are genuinely valuable, but they don't address the structural economic challenge of rural Iowa, which is population decline in agricultural communities. The communities that lose farmland to data center development don't necessarily have the population or the technical workforce to fill the operations roles the campus creates. The economic benefit and the economic disruption land in different zip codes.

## Land Conservation & Community Impact

The soil carbon dimension of agricultural land conversion is underappreciated in data center coverage. Class I Iowa topsoil contains roughly 50 to 100 metric tons of organic carbon per hectare accumulated over post-glacial soil development — carbon that is sequestered in stable forms within the soil aggregate structure and the microbial biomass. When that soil is excavated, graded, and sealed under concrete, that carbon is eventually released to the atmosphere through oxidation of exposed organic matter. The data center that replaces the field then gets to claim renewable energy credits for its power consumption. The carbon cost of the land conversion is unaccounted in any emissions framework I've seen applied to data center projects.

Iowa's county assessors and agricultural extension offices track farmland values closely — Iowa farmland hit record prices in 2023 and 2024, with Class I ground trading above $15,000 per acre in some counties. When that land converts to data center use, it leaves the agricultural land base permanently. It doesn't come back when the data center is decommissioned in 30 years, because decommissioning leaves contaminated soil from diesel storage, transformer oils, and battery systems that requires remediation before the land can return to any agricultural use. The permanent land conversion cost — in food production capacity, soil carbon, hydrological function, and community character — doesn't appear in any project financial model. It's borne by the land and the people who live near it.

Iowa should add a prime agricultural soil exclusion zone to its data center incentive program. That's not a radical position. It's what a state that takes both economic development and land stewardship seriously would do. The incentives can still work. They just shouldn't work on the best soil in the world.
