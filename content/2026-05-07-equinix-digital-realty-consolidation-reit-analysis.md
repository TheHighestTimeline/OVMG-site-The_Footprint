## The Ecological Development Brief

Digital Realty's Q1 revenue: $1.41 billion. Consensus: $1.47 billion. Shares dropped 8.3% on the print, recovered about half by end of week. The market overreacted, as it usually does. But the pressure underneath the miss is real and it's been building for longer than one bad quarter.

The pressure has a name. Hyperscaler self-build. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon have collectively announced $340 billion in owned data center capex over the next three years. Every square foot they construct themselves is a square foot they're not leasing from Digital Realty or anyone else in the wholesale colo space. That math has been visible for two years. Q1 confirmed it's now showing up in the earnings — and operators who built their business around hyperscaler anchor tenants are learning that their best customers have become their most capable competitors.

What I find most telling isn't the miss itself. It's what the miss reveals about the ecological bets these REITs have already made. Data center campuses aren't built on quarterly cycles — they're built on 20- to 30-year infrastructure horizons, on land that was cleared, graded, and committed for that full period. When a hyperscaler moves out of a wholesale colo facility and into a campus it owns, the operator is left holding land, power contracts, and water rights sized for a tenant who's gone. The land conversion that happened to build that facility doesn't reverse. The watershed alteration doesn't heal. The community that was told the facility would bring stable long-term tenancy doesn't get that promise back.

## Water & Cooling Impact Analysis

Equinix operates over 240 data centers across 33 countries. Digital Realty runs more than 300. A combined entity would control something like 400-plus facilities with a water footprint representing one of the largest single concentrations of industrial water dependency in the technology sector. I haven't seen that sentence in any analyst note on the consolidation thesis. It should be the lead.

Both companies publish sustainability reports with WUE figures, and both deserve credit for disclosing more than most of the industry does. But WUE is a volume ratio — not an ecological impact assessment. It doesn't tell you which watersheds those withdrawals are coming from, what the ambient stress levels of those systems are, or what the cumulative chemical load from cooling discharge looks like against their buffering capacity. Equinix's global WUE average gets calculated the same way whether the water is coming from drought-stressed Northern Virginia or a facility in Scotland with abundant precipitation. Those aren't equivalent situations. The single number says they are.

Equinix's retail model does have a genuine ecological argument that the company is slow to make clearly: shared infrastructure at high utilization is more resource-efficient per unit of compute than single-tenant campuses. A well-utilized colo facility serving 50 customers shares cooling plant, power infrastructure, and mechanical systems across those loads — lower per-rack water and energy overhead than 50 companies each running dedicated infrastructure. That argument is real. Whether Equinix's actual utilization rates support it isn't something their public disclosures currently allow anyone outside the company to verify.

## Power Grid & Emissions Profile

The case for Equinix specifically isn't raw megawatt capacity. It's the interconnection revenue — approaching $1.2 billion annually, 80%-plus gross margins, almost entirely insulated from hyperscaler self-build economics. Because you can't replicate Equinix NY5 in Secaucus by building your own campus. Ten thousand companies cross-connect there. Your financial counterparties are there. Your cloud on-ramps. Your CDN peering partners. You pay the port fees and premium rack rates because routing latency-sensitive traffic across the public internet isn't operationally acceptable. That stickiness is structural. It's why Equinix's book looks different from Digital Realty's under market stress.

Both companies are operating in a world where power scarcity — not capital — is the binding growth constraint. Northern Virginia, Dublin, Singapore: all have seen interconnection moratoriums or significant throttling of new capacity approvals in the past 18 months. PJM's queue for new large-load customers runs past 2030. Operators who locked power agreements in 2022 and 2023 have an advantage that isn't replicable today at any price. That timing advantage has an environmental dimension worth noting: facilities built when interconnection was easier are often in markets with more carbon-intensive grids than newer markets where renewables have expanded. Getting a good power deal and getting a clean power deal aren't always the same thing.

Digital Realty's wholesale book tends toward higher facility utilization — large single tenants push campuses closer to rated capacity, improving PUE. But high utilization from one tenant also means high vacancy risk when they leave. An underutilized data center isn't ecologically free. It still draws standby power, runs cooling at idle, maintains mechanical systems for a load that's no longer there.

## Land Conservation & Community Impact

The consolidation argument — Equinix and Digital Realty combining into the unambiguous global leader across retail and wholesale — is operationally coherent. Scale in power procurement, land acquisition, and equipment pricing produces real efficiencies. Regulatory obstacles are significant: antitrust scrutiny in the U.S. and EU is tightening. But the industrial logic doesn't go away because regulators are skeptical.

What's consistently missing is any accounting of the combined land footprint and its ecological context. The Ashburn corridor in Northern Virginia is the densest concentration of data center infrastructure on earth. It's also a land conversion story that's documented in local planning records and has never received the ecological audit its scale warrants. A combined entity with a meaningful share of that footprint would have both the data and the obligation to conduct that audit. Whether it would is a different question.

The REIT shakeout is a financial story with an ecological subtext the industry hasn't learned to tell. Operators holding the better hand aren't just those with stronger interconnection revenue — they're the ones who secured land and power before the market tightened, which means their environmental obligations are already embedded in communities and watersheds that have been hosting them for years. That history deserves an accounting. Quarterly earnings calls don't provide it. But somewhere, someone has to.
